## Key Findings

### 1. Revenue Decline Is Not Demand-Driven
- Overall revenue declined despite stable website traffic.
- Average Order Value (AOV) remained consistent over time.
- This rules out pricing issues, discount strategy, or reduced customer demand as primary causes.

### 2. Order Volume Showed No Structural Drop
- Month-over-month order counts fluctuated mildly but showed no sustained downward trend.
- Conversion volume remained broadly stable, indicating that customers continued placing orders.

### 3. Payment Failures Were Not the Primary Driver
- Payment failure rates stayed relatively stable (~7–10%) across months.
- No sharp spikes or deterioration aligned with the revenue decline period.
- Payment gateway issues were therefore not the main contributor to revenue loss.

### 4. Significant Post-Payment Fulfillment Failures
- 20–30% of successfully paid orders failed to convert into delivered orders.
- This represents a major breakdown after revenue was already captured at checkout.
- These failures directly translated into unrealized or reversed revenue.

### 5. Returns Were the Largest Source of Revenue Leakage
- Among non-delivered orders, returns accounted for the majority share.
- Pre-delivery cancellations formed the second-largest contributor.
- Logistics and fulfillment inefficiencies outweighed customer-side or payment-side issues.

### 6. Revenue Leakage Is Operational in Nature
- The root cause of revenue decline lies in post-payment execution rather than acquisition, pricing, or checkout.
- Operational failures in fulfillment and returns management are the dominant drivers of lost revenue.
 
## Root Cause Conclusion
The revenue decline is primarily driven by post-payment operational failures rather than demand-side or monetization issues. High return rates and fulfillment inefficiencies prevent a substantial portion of successfully paid orders from converting into realized revenue. Improving post-payment execution presents the highest opportunity for revenue recovery.
